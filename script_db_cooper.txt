CHAPTER 1 - THE HOOK (0:00-2:30)

November 24th, 1971. The day before Thanksgiving. Portland International Airport, Oregon.

A man walks up to the Northwest Orient Airlines counter. He's wearing a dark suit, a white shirt, a thin black tie. Mid-forties, maybe. Polite. Unremarkable. He pays cash for a one-way ticket to Seattle. Twenty dollars.

The name he gives is Dan Cooper.

Thirty minutes later, that name will become one of the most famous aliases in criminal history. Because the man in seat 18-C is about to do something no one has ever done before - and no one has ever done since.

He is going to hijack a Boeing 727, collect two hundred thousand dollars in ransom, strap on a parachute, and jump out of the back of the aircraft into a freezing storm over the Pacific Northwest.

And then he is going to vanish. Completely. Forever.

The FBI would spend forty-five years chasing him. They would investigate over a thousand suspects. They would dig through riverbanks, drag lakes, analyze tie clips and cigarette butts and parachute fibers. And in 2016, they would finally close the case - with no answer.

This is the story of D.B. Cooper. The only unsolved skyjacking in American history.

CHAPTER 2 - THE GOLDEN AGE OF SKYJACKING (2:30-6:00)

To understand how a man could walk onto a plane with a bomb in a briefcase, you have to understand what flying was like in 1971. Because it was nothing like today.

There were no metal detectors. No X-ray machines. No ID checks. You could buy a ticket with cash under any name you liked, walk to the gate, and board. Airlines actively fought against security screening - they thought it would insult passengers and slow down boarding. Flying was glamorous, casual, and completely unguarded.

And criminals had noticed. Between 1968 and 1972, the United States experienced what historians now call the golden age of skyjacking. Over one hundred and thirty American planes were hijacked in those five years. At the peak, planes were being taken more than once a week. It sounds unbelievable, but hijackings had become so routine that crews were trained to simply comply, fly to Cuba - the destination for the vast majority of hijackers - refuel, and fly home. Airlines kept landing charts for Havana in every cockpit, just in case.

Most of those hijackers wanted transportation - a free ride to Cuba - or made political statements. They were caught, or they disappeared into exile. What almost none of them wanted was money. And none of them had ever tried to leave a plane in mid-air.

That November, the man calling himself Dan Cooper was about to invent an entirely new category of crime. And the era he did it in - the unguarded gates, the compliant crews, the routine of it all - was the only window in history when his plan was even possible.

Within a few years, that window would be sealed shut. Partly because of him.

CHAPTER 3 - FLIGHT 305 (6:00-10:00)

Let's go back to that afternoon.

Northwest Orient Flight 305 was a short hop - Portland to Seattle, about thirty minutes in the air. The aircraft was a Boeing 727-100, and that detail matters more than almost anything else in this story. We'll come back to it.

The flight was barely a third full. Thirty-six passengers, six crew. The man calling himself Dan Cooper took seat 18-C, near the rear of the cabin. He ordered a bourbon and soda. He paid for it. Witnesses would later describe him the same way, over and over: calm, polite, quiet.

Shortly after takeoff, at around 3 PM, Cooper passed a note to the flight attendant seated nearest him - a twenty-three-year-old named Florence Schaffner. She assumed it was a phone number. Lonely businessmen did that all the time. She dropped it into her purse without reading it.

Cooper leaned over and said, quietly: "Miss, you'd better look at that note. I have a bomb."

The note said essentially the same thing, in neat printed letters. Schaffner did exactly what her training told her to do - she stayed calm and asked to see it. Cooper opened his briefcase just enough for her to see red cylinders, wires, and a battery. Whether it was a real bomb, nobody knows to this day. It didn't matter. It looked real enough.

His demands were specific, and this is where the case starts to get strange. He didn't ask for a million dollars. He didn't ask to be flown to another country, which is what almost every other hijacker of that era did. He asked for exactly two hundred thousand dollars in what he called "negotiable American currency," four parachutes - two main, two reserve - and a fuel truck waiting in Seattle.

Think about the parachute demand for a second. Four parachutes. By asking for four, he implied he might force crew members to jump with him. That meant the authorities couldn't risk giving him sabotaged chutes. It was a quiet, brutal piece of logic - and it worked.

The pilot, Captain William Scott, relayed the demands to Northwest Orient. The airline's president made the call within minutes: pay him. Two hundred thousand dollars in 1971 is roughly one and a half million in today's money - but a destroyed aircraft full of passengers would cost far more.

The flight circled Puget Sound for nearly two hours while everything was arranged on the ground. Here's another detail that tells you something about this man: during those two hours, Cooper was, by every account, relaxed. He ordered a second bourbon. He paid his drink tab. He offered to request meals for the crew. One flight attendant, Tina Mucklow, spent most of the flight sitting next to him, and later said he seemed rational, thoughtful - never cruel, never nervous.

The passengers, meanwhile, had no idea a hijacking was even happening. They were told the delay was a minor mechanical issue.

At 5:39 PM, Flight 305 landed in Seattle.

CHAPTER 4 - THE EXCHANGE (10:00-13:30)

On the tarmac at Seattle-Tacoma airport, everything happened the way Cooper ordered it to happen.

An employee delivered the money - ten thousand twenty-dollar bills, twenty-one pounds of cash, packed in a canvas bag. Crucially, the FBI had done one smart thing on short notice: every single bill had been run through a Recordak machine, photographing the serial numbers. Ten thousand traceable bills. Remember that.

The parachutes arrived. Cooper released all thirty-six passengers, plus two of the flight attendants. He kept the pilots, one flight engineer, and Tina Mucklow on board.

While the plane refueled, Cooper laid out the second phase of his plan to the cockpit crew, and this is where investigators would later focus enormous attention - because these instructions revealed real knowledge.

He ordered the plane flown toward Mexico City. But not just that. He specified: altitude no higher than ten thousand feet. Landing gear down. Flaps at fifteen degrees. Cabin unpressurized. Airspeed under two hundred knots.

Every one of those settings has a purpose. Together, they turn a commercial jet into something you can survive jumping out of. That's not something you improvise. Either this man had aviation training, or he had studied this obsessively.

And then, the reason he chose this exact aircraft: the Boeing 727 had a rear exit - the aft airstairs - a staircase that folded down from the tail, beneath the engines. It could be opened in flight. Almost no other passenger jet in the world had that. Cooper knew it. He even corrected the crew when they claimed it couldn't be lowered mid-air.

At 7:36 PM, Flight 305 took off again into the night and into a rainstorm, headed south along a flight corridor called Victor 23. Two F-106 fighter jets shadowed it from a distance - too fast, ironically, to fly as slow as the 727. They kept losing it.

Cooper ordered everyone into the cockpit and told them to stay there. Tina Mucklow's last image of him, as she closed the cockpit door, was of a quiet man in a suit, tying the money bag to himself.

At 8:00 PM, a warning light flashed in the cockpit: the aft airstairs had been activated.

At approximately 8:13 PM, the crew felt it - a sudden upward bump of pressure, the plane physically reacting as weight left it and the stairs snapped back.

Somewhere over the dark, storm-soaked forests of southwest Washington state, a man in a business suit and loafers, with twenty-one pounds of cash tied to his body, stepped off the back of a jet into a two-hundred-mile-an-hour wind, rain, and a temperature - with wind chill - somewhere around seventy degrees below zero Fahrenheit.

When Flight 305 landed in Reno, Nevada, the aft stairs still hanging open, agents stormed the plane.

The cabin was empty. Dan Cooper was gone.

CHAPTER 5 - THE MANHUNT (13:30-18:00)

The FBI called the case NORJAK - Northwest Hijacking. It would stay open for forty-five years.

Let's start with the name. The hijacker called himself Dan Cooper. So why does the entire world know him as D.B. Cooper? Because in the chaos of the first days, police briefly looked at a Portland man with the initials D.B. - he was quickly cleared, he had nothing to do with it - but a wire reporter ran the initials, and they stuck. The most famous name in American crime mystery is literally a typo.

The search itself was enormous. The problem was: nobody knew exactly where he jumped. The crew felt the pressure bump at 8:13 PM, but pinpointing the plane's exact position at that second, in 1971, on a stormy night, left a search zone of hundreds of square miles of dense forest, ravines, and rivers - some of the roughest terrain in the country.

Hundreds of soldiers from Fort Lewis walked the woods. Helicopters flew grid patterns. The FBI ran door-to-door interviews across rural Washington. They dragged lakes. In spring 1972, they did it all again.

They found nothing. No parachute. No body. No briefcase. No money. It was as if the sky had swallowed him.

Meanwhile, the FBI worked the evidence from the plane. Cooper had left things behind: a black clip-on tie with a mother-of-pearl tie clip, eight cigarette butts, and the two parachutes he didn't take. And here investigators noticed something odd - of the two reserve chutes, Cooper had taken the one that was actually a non-functional training dummy, sewn shut, and left the working one behind. Some read that as proof he wasn't a real expert. Others point out he took a functioning main chute, and the dummy reserve may simply not have mattered to his plan.

The tie became one of the most analyzed objects in FBI history. Decades later, scientists ran particle analysis on it and found traces of pure titanium and other rare metals - materials that in 1971 were mostly found in aerospace and chemical manufacturing plants. It's one of the strongest hints we have about who this man might have been: possibly someone who worked in, or around, the aerospace industry.

And there was the money. Ten thousand recorded serial numbers, distributed to banks, casinos, businesses across the country. In the years that followed, not one single bill ever surfaced in circulation. Not one. Two hundred thousand dollars, and the man who risked his life for it apparently never spent a cent.

CHAPTER 6 - THE MONEY ON THE RIVERBANK (18:00-21:30)

For nine years, the case went cold. And then an eight-year-old boy went camping.

February 1980. Tena Bar, a sandy stretch of the Columbia River bank, north of Vancouver, Washington. A family named Ingram is setting up for a picnic. Their son, Brian, is smoothing out a spot in the sand for a campfire - and his hand hits something.

Three bundles of twenty-dollar bills. Rotted, fused together, corners crumbling. Still held by rubber bands.

Five thousand eight hundred dollars. The FBI checked the serial numbers. Every single bill was from the Cooper ransom.

This should have solved everything. Instead, it broke the case open in the worst way - because the money was found in the wrong place.

Tena Bar is not in the primary calculated drop zone. Depending on which flight-path analysis you trust, it's miles away - and critically, it sits where the favored drop zones would not naturally wash debris. The rubber bands were still intact, and rubber bands disintegrate in water within a couple of years - which suggests to some analysts the money arrived at that beach relatively soon after the hijacking, not after a decade of drifting. Yet the bills' edges were rounded in ways consistent with long water exposure.

Every theory has a problem. If Cooper landed near there - that contradicts the flight path. If the money floated there - the bundles should have separated. If someone planted it - who, and why only fifty-eight hundred dollars?

Scientists have studied sand layers, dredging records, even the algae between the bills. To this day, there is no accepted explanation for how Cooper's money got to Tena Bar. The single biggest physical clue in the case only deepened the mystery.

The FBI dug up that beach and found nothing else. The rest of the money - one hundred ninety-four thousand dollars - has never been seen.

CHAPTER 7 - THE SUSPECTS (21:30-26:30)

Over four and a half decades, the FBI looked at more than a thousand people. Let's be clear about something before we go through this: the Bureau cleared or could never confirm every single one of them. No one has ever been charged. Everything from here is documented investigation, not accusation.

There was Richard Floyd McCoy, who five months after Cooper pulled off an almost identical hijacking - a 727, a ransom, a parachute escape - and was caught within days. The similarities were so strong that many agents liked him for Cooper. But McCoy didn't match the physical descriptions given by the flight attendants, and the FBI ultimately ruled him out for the 1971 jump. He died in 1974 in a shootout after escaping prison.

There was Duane Weber, whose widow reported that on his deathbed in 1995 he told her "I'm Dan Cooper." A compelling story - but his fingerprints didn't match prints lifted from the plane, and DNA testing later excluded him against a partial profile taken from the tie.

There was Kenneth Christiansen, a former paratrooper who - and this is the detail that makes everyone sit up - worked for Northwest Orient Airlines itself. He knew 727s. His brother became convinced after his death. The FBI looked, and concluded he didn't fit key witness descriptions.

There was Barbara Dayton, a recreational pilot who had lived until 1969 as Robert Dayton, and who reportedly claimed to friends to have done it dressed as a man - then recanted. Never substantiated.

And there was Robert Rackstraw, a former Army pilot with a colorful criminal history, pushed hard by a team of independent investigators in the 2010s. The FBI had looked at him back in the seventies - and let him go. He denied it until his death in 2019.

Here's the honest truth the FBI itself arrived at: the witness descriptions, the physical evidence, and the DNA never lined up with any suspect well enough. And there's a darker, quieter possibility that many agents privately favored - that Cooper was never in any suspect file, because Cooper did not survive the night.

Consider what he jumped into: darkness, heavy rain, near-freezing air at altitude, wind blast strong enough to rip shoes off, over terrain that kills prepared hikers in daylight. He wore a suit and street shoes. If he died in a ravine in that wilderness, animals and forty winters could erase a body completely. In that version, the answer isn't hidden in a file. It's just out there, under the moss, and always has been.

But then - where's the parachute? Searches never found it. And how did the money get to Tena Bar?

Every road in this case ends the same way: at a question mark.

CHAPTER 8 - WHY WE CANNOT LET GO (26:30-30:30)

In July 2016, the FBI officially suspended active investigation of NORJAK. Forty-five years, one of the longest-running investigations in Bureau history - closed without an answer. The evidence sits preserved in case something new ever surfaces. Citizen investigators - they call themselves the Cooperites - are still working it today, running particle analyses and flight-path recalculations the original agents could only dream of.

So why does this case refuse to die?

Partly because it's clean. Nobody was hurt. Cooper was polite, he paid his bar tab, he released the passengers. The FBI never romanticized him - what he did was dangerous and criminal, a bomb threat is a bomb threat - but the public did, almost immediately. Songs were written. Bars in the Northwest still hold Cooper-themed nights every November.

Partly because it's the last of its kind. Cooper's jump triggered real consequences: within a few years, aircraft were fitted with a device - literally called the Cooper vane - that physically prevents rear airstairs from opening in flight. Airport security screening expanded across the country. The loophole he found closed behind him. No one can ever do it again, which makes him permanently unique.

And partly, I think, because of what the mystery lets us imagine. Every other criminal from that era was eventually caught, or named, or found dead. Cooper alone gets to remain a silhouette - a quiet man in a suit who beat the system once and stepped off the world's back porch into the dark.

The rational answer is probably the boring one: a desperate man with some aviation knowledge overestimated himself, died on impact or of exposure within hours, and the forest kept the secret. The evidence - the unspent money, the missing parachute, the silence of fifty years - fits a dead man better than a living legend.

Whoever he was - an aerospace engineer, a paratrooper, a nobody with a library card and a grudge - he took one piece of information the world had missed, about a staircase on the back of a Boeing 727, and turned it into the perfect vanishing act.

Fifty years later, we're still standing on that riverbank, holding a handful of rotted twenties, asking the dark treeline the same question.
