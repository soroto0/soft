Deep in the fog of Northern California, the tallest trees on Earth have been keeping a secret. Coast redwoods can live for more than two thousand years. Some of them were already ancient when Rome was still an empire.

A single redwood drinks hundreds of liters of water every day. But here is the surprising part. Much of that water never comes from the ground. The trees catch the fog itself, combing moisture out of the air with millions of tiny needles.

Below the surface, the story gets even stranger. Redwood roots are shallow, only a few meters deep. Instead of growing down, they grow outward, wrapping around the roots of their neighbors. A redwood forest is not a crowd of single trees. It is one connected network, holding itself up through every storm.

And when an old redwood finally falls, it does not really die. New trunks rise from its roots, growing in a circle around the fallen giant. Scientists call it a fairy ring. The forest, it turns out, remembers every tree it has ever lost.
